# References — curated list (with summaries)

This file collects 12 curated peer-reviewed papers relevant to dopamine neurobiology, stimulant addiction and withdrawal, anhedonia, and recovery processes. Each entry includes a full citation, a 1–2 sentence summary of findings, and a DOI or stable URL.

1. Koob, G. F., & Volkow, N. D. (2016). Neurobiology of addiction: a neurocircuitry analysis. The Lancet Psychiatry, 3(8), 760–773. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(16)00104-8
   - Summary: Synthesizes animal and human data into a staged model of addiction (binge/intoxication; withdrawal/negative affect; preoccupation/anticipation) and maps these stages onto interacting brain circuits including reward, stress, and executive control. This framework highlights how dysregulated dopaminergic signaling interacts with other systems to drive compulsive drug use and informs targets for recovery.
   - Free full text: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6135092/

2. Volkow, N. D., Michaelides, M., & Baler, R. (2019). The neuroscience of drug reward and addiction. Physiological Reviews, 99(4), 2115–2140. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1152/physrev.00014.2018
   - Summary: Comprehensive review of molecular, cellular, and circuit-level adaptations produced by addictive drugs, integrating PET imaging, preclinical models, and clinical observations; emphasizes both phasic and tonic dopamine signaling changes. Useful as an up-to-date synthesis of mechanisms relevant to withdrawal, tolerance, and long-term recovery.
   - PDF: https://journals.physiology.org/doi/pdf/10.1152/physrev.00014.2018

3. Volkow, N. D., Fowler, J. S., & Wang, G.-J. (2003). The addicted human brain: insights from imaging studies. Journal of Clinical Investigation, 111(10), 1444–1451. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI18533
   - Summary: Early landmark review of PET and functional imaging findings in substance use disorders, reporting reduced striatal D2 receptor availability and frontal cortical hypofunction in addicted individuals. These imaging biomarkers are frequently cited as evidence that dopamine system deficits and impaired executive control underlie addiction and its behavioral manifestations.
   - Article: https://www.jci.org/articles/view/18533

4. Robinson, T. E., & Berridge, K. C. (1993). The neural basis of drug craving: an incentive-sensitization theory of addiction. Brain Research Reviews, 18(3), 247–291. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/0165-0173(93)90013-P
   - Summary: Proposes that repeated drug exposure sensitizes neural systems that attribute incentive salience to drug-associated cues, producing intense ‘wanting’ (craving) even when hedonic responses may not increase. This influential theory separates motivational drive from pleasure and helps explain persistent relapse risk.

5. Nestler, E. J. (2001). Molecular neurobiology of addiction. The American Journal on Addictions, 10(3), 201–217. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/105504901750364848
   - Summary: Reviews intracellular signaling pathways and transcriptional regulators (e.g., cAMP, CREB, ΔFosB) implicated in long-term neuroplastic changes after drug exposure, linking molecular adaptations to behavioral sensitization and relapse vulnerability. These mechanisms are central to understanding persistent alterations in dopamine-related circuits and potential molecular targets for recovery.

6. Nestler, E. J. (2005). Is there a common molecular pathway for addiction? Nature Neuroscience, 8(11), 1445–1449. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/nn1578
   - Summary: Discusses evidence for shared molecular adaptations across different classes of addictive drugs and highlights key transcriptional and epigenetic mechanisms that may mediate long-lasting changes in reward circuitry. The paper frames potential convergent targets for therapeutic strategies aimed at normalizing dopaminergic function.

7. Belujon, P., & Grace, A. A. (2017). Dopamine system dysregulation in major depressive disorders. International Journal of Neuropsychopharmacology, 20(12), 1036–1046. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1093/ijnp/pyx056
   - Summary: Reviews preclinical and clinical evidence linking altered dopamine transmission (including firing patterns and receptor function) to symptoms of depression and anhedonia, emphasizing how changes in mesolimbic dopamine can drive motivational deficits. Relevant for connecting mechanistic dopamine research to clinical presentations of anhedonia and recovery trajectories.
   - Free full text: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5716179/

8. Robinson, T. E., & Berridge, K. C. (2008). The incentive-sensitization theory of addiction: some current issues. Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B: Biological Sciences, 363(1507), 3137–3146. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.2008.0093
   - Summary: Updates and defends the incentive-sensitization model in light of new data, addressing criticisms and clarifying the distinctions between ‘liking’ and ‘wanting’ systems and their neural substrates. This paper is useful for interpreting how cue-driven dopamine responses persist after drug cessation and contribute to relapse.

9. Volkow, N. D., Wang, G.-J., Fowler, J. S., Tomasi, D., & Telang, F. (2011). Addiction: beyond dopamine reward circuitry. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 108(37), 15037–15042. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1010654108
   - Summary: Argues that addiction involves broader network dysfunction including prefrontal cortical systems responsible for self-control and decision-making, not just mesolimbic dopamine. The paper highlights imaging evidence that impaired executive function interacts with dopamine alterations to sustain addictive behaviors.

10. Nutt, D. J., Lingford-Hughes, A., Erritzoe, D., & Stokes, P. R. A. (2015). The dopamine theory of addiction: 40 years of highs and lows. Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 16(5), 305–312. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn3939
   - Summary: Critical appraisal of dopamine-centric models, reviewing supportive and contradictory evidence and emphasizing the complexity of addiction involving multiple neurotransmitters and circuits. Useful for a balanced perspective on what dopamine explains well and where other systems must be considered in recovery.

11. Dresp-Langley, B. (2023). From reward to anhedonia—dopamine function in the global mental health context. Biomedicines, 11(9), 2469. DOI: https://doi.org/10.3390/biomedicines11092469
   - Summary: Recent synthesis discussing mechanisms by which dopaminergic dysfunction can shift reward processing toward anhedonia and implications for mental health; connects molecular, circuit, and clinical perspectives and notes translational considerations for interventions.
   - Article: https://www.mdpi.com/2227-9059/11/9/2469

12. Goldstein, R. Z., & Volkow, N. D. (2002). Drug addiction and its underlying neurobiological basis: neuroimaging evidence for the involvement of the frontal cortex. American Journal of Psychiatry, 159(10), 1642–1652. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.159.10.1642
   - Summary: Reviews imaging studies implicating prefrontal cortical dysfunction (e.g., reduced metabolism, altered activation) in impaired decision-making and inhibitory control among addiction patients, linking these deficits to relapse risk and treatment challenges.
